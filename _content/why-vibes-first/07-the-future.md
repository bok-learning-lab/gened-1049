# The Future of Vibes-First Learning

## Beyond Coding

The argument so far has been about coding — about why starting with AI-generated code, verifying it, and building understanding iteratively is a sound approach to computational literacy. But the deeper claim is larger than coding. The skills that vibes-first coding develops may be among the most important skills for humans to cultivate in the coming decades, because the condition it describes — working effectively with powerful systems you don't fully understand — is rapidly becoming the default condition of modern life.

## The Expanding Opacity

We are entering an era in which more and more of the systems we depend on are opaque. This has been true for a long time — most people couldn't explain how a combustion engine works, or how a power grid is balanced, or how TCP/IP routes a packet — but the pace and stakes of opacity are increasing. AI systems now draft legal briefs, generate medical diagnoses, write code, create images, summarize research, and make recommendations that shape what we read, buy, and believe. In each case, the system's internal reasoning is not fully legible to its users, and often not fully legible to its creators.

This is not a temporary condition. As AI systems become more capable, they become more opaque, not less — Latour's paradox of black-boxing applied at civilizational scale. The question is not whether we'll work with systems we don't fully understand. We already do. The question is whether we'll do it well.

## The Skills That Matter

If vibes-first coding teaches anything generalizable, it's a set of dispositions and practices for navigating opacity responsibly. These are worth naming, because they extend far beyond coding.

**Attention to detail.** When you can't understand a system from the inside, you must attend carefully to its outputs. Does this look right? Does it match what I expected? Is there something off that I can't quite name yet? The capacity for sustained, careful attention — noticing what's actually there rather than what you expect to see — becomes crucial when you're evaluating artifacts produced by systems that are fluent, confident, and sometimes wrong. In coding, this means reading outputs carefully and testing edge cases. In the broader world, it means reading AI-generated text with the same critical eye you'd bring to any source, noticing when something plausible is actually nonsensical.

**Care.** There is a moral dimension to working with powerful tools. If you can generate code in minutes that would have taken days to write, the temptation is to generate carelessly — to accept the first output, skip verification, and move on. The same temptation applies to AI-generated writing, analysis, images, and recommendations. Care means slowing down enough to verify, taking responsibility for what you produce even when a machine helped produce it, and being honest about what you do and don't understand. Care is what separates tool *use* from tool *abuse*.

**Aesthetic judgment.** This may sound surprising in the context of coding, but it matters more than people think. When you look at a piece of AI-generated code — or AI-generated prose, or an AI-generated image — and you feel that something is off, that sense is aesthetic judgment. It's pattern recognition operating below the level of articulation, the felt sense that something doesn't cohere, doesn't fit, doesn't look like what a careful human would produce. As AI-generated artifacts proliferate, the ability to distinguish quality from fluent mediocrity becomes a kind of connoisseurship. Not everyone needs to be able to articulate *why* something is wrong, but the capacity to sense that it *is* wrong — and to act on that sense — is invaluable. This is, incidentally, a skill that humanities training develops exceptionally well.

**Specification clarity.** The ability to say precisely what you want — and what you don't want — becomes more important as AI systems become more powerful. A vague prompt produces vague results. A precise specification, with examples, constraints, and success criteria, produces dramatically better results. This skill is really a form of thinking clearly: knowing what you actually want (not just what you think you want), anticipating edge cases, and communicating intent without ambiguity. It is as relevant to writing a research question as it is to prompting an AI.

**Epistemic humility.** Knowing what you don't know — and being honest about it — has always been a scholarly virtue. In a world of increasingly capable AI, it becomes a survival skill. The danger is not that people will use AI tools. The danger is that they'll use them without awareness of their limitations, trusting outputs they haven't verified, claiming understanding they don't have, and building on foundations they haven't checked. Vibes-first coding, with its emphasis on verification and incremental trust, cultivates the habit of asking "How do I know this is right?" rather than assuming that fluent output is correct output.

**Iterative refinement.** Almost nothing works perfectly the first time — not code, not prose, not arguments, not designs. The willingness to treat a first attempt as a draft, to test it, find its failures, and improve it, is a disposition that vibes-first coding builds by necessity (because AI code *will* fail in some cases) and that transfers to nearly every domain of intellectual and creative work.

## Why Humanities Training Becomes More Valuable, Not Less

There is a common anxiety that AI will make humanities skills obsolete — that if machines can write, analyze, and interpret, then the human capacity for those things loses value. The argument of these documents runs in exactly the opposite direction.

As AI-generated artifacts proliferate — code, text, images, analysis, recommendations — the skills that become *more* valuable are precisely the skills that humanistic training develops: the ability to evaluate artifacts you didn't create, to notice what's wrong before you can say why, to hold multiple interpretations simultaneously, to attend to nuance and context, to distinguish genuine insight from fluent nonsense, and to build understanding iteratively from encounter rather than from first principles.

The literary critic who can spot a weak argument, the musicologist who can hear when a passage doesn't resolve properly, the art historian who can see when a composition lacks coherence — these are people who have spent years developing exactly the kind of evaluative, interpretive, and aesthetic judgment that matters most in a world saturated with machine-generated artifacts.

This is not to say that STEM skills become less important. They don't. But the notion that humanistic training is a luxury, irrelevant to the "real" demands of a technological era, has it precisely backwards. The more powerful our generative tools become, the more we need people who can evaluate what those tools produce — and evaluation is what the humanities have been training people to do for centuries.

## The Vibes-First Disposition as General Method

Step back from coding specifically and consider the vibes-first progression as a general method for learning in conditions of opacity:

1. Encounter a complex artifact you don't fully understand.
2. Develop intuitions through interaction — a felt sense of what's working and what isn't.
3. Test those intuitions against evidence.
4. Develop vocabulary to articulate what you're noticing.
5. Build understanding iteratively, going deeper where it matters and abstracting where it doesn't.
6. Maintain epistemic humility — know what you've verified and what you're taking on trust.

This is not only a method for learning to code. It's a method for navigating a world in which the systems around us — technological, institutional, informational — are increasingly complex, increasingly capable, and increasingly opaque. The person who can work responsibly in those conditions, who can verify without pretending to fully understand, who can iterate toward correctness without demanding certainty at every step — that person is well-equipped for the decades ahead.

## What We Might Lose

This is speculative, and intellectual honesty requires acknowledging the risks. If vibes-first becomes the dominant way people interact with AI systems, there are things we might lose.

We might lose depth. If operational competence is always sufficient, if you can always just ask the AI to fix it, the motivation to understand deeply — to go from Level 1 to Level 2 or Level 3 — may weaken. There's a difference between choosing not to go deeper (because you don't need to) and being unable to go deeper (because you never developed the foundations). The vibes-first approach depends on the possibility of depth when it's needed. If the on-ramp is so comfortable that no one takes the exit toward deeper understanding, that's a problem.

We might lose the capacity for independent thought in domains where AI assistance becomes pervasive. Not because AI makes us dumber, but because the path of least resistance is to accept the first plausible output. Critical evaluation is effortful. Verification takes time. The temptation to skip it grows as AI outputs get better — "good enough" may quietly erode into "I stopped checking."

We might lose certain forms of knowledge that only come from the slow, frustrating process of building from scratch. There is something that programmers know from having written code from nothing — a feel for the machine, an intuition about what's possible and what's hard — that may not develop through vibes-first learning alone. The same may be true in other domains: the musician who only ever played by ear may lack something the classically trained musician has, even if both can perform.

These are real risks, and they deserve ongoing attention. The response is not to abandon vibes-first approaches but to design them carefully — to build in the motivation for depth, to cultivate the habit of verification, to create pathways from operational competence to conceptual and implementation understanding for those who need them.

## A Speculative Conclusion

The bet of vibes-first learning is that in a world of increasingly powerful and increasingly opaque systems, the most important human skills are not implementation skills (the machines are taking those over) but judgment skills: attention, care, aesthetic sense, specification clarity, epistemic humility, and iterative refinement. These are skills that can be taught, that transfer across domains, and that — notably — align closely with what humanistic education has always cultivated.

If this bet is right, then the workshop you're participating in is not just about learning to build a web app with AI help. It's practice for a way of being in the world — a way of engaging with powerful, partially opaque, rapidly evolving systems with enough skill to use them well, enough humility to know your limits, and enough care to take responsibility for what you produce.

Whether that bet pays off is an empirical question. We'll know more in five years, more in ten. But the skills it cultivates — attention, verification, iterative understanding, intellectual honesty — are valuable regardless of how the technology develops. They're valuable because they're the skills of a careful mind engaging with a complex world. That has always been worth learning.
