# THE VIBES-FIRST MANIFESTO

*For a New Computational Literacy*

We have watched long enough from the galleries of tradition. We have sat through enough lectures on syntax before substance, fundamentals before function, theory before touch. We have endured the lie that understanding must precede use — that you must disassemble the engine before you are permitted to drive.

**We declare that era over.**

We stand at a threshold. On one side: the old priesthood of code, its gates locked with semicolons, its temples accessible only to those who memorized the catechism of data structures and algorithmic complexity. On the other: a new literacy, wild and rigorous, born from the collision of human language and machine intelligence.

We choose the threshold. We choose the leap.

## I.

**We declare that the bottleneck has moved.** It used to be: *Can you write syntactically correct code?* Now it is: *Can you say what you mean, verify what you got, and iterate until it serves you?* The ability to specify intent, test outcomes, and refine through cycles of encounter and judgment — this is the literacy of our moment. The old question — "Can you write this from scratch?" — is a question for a world that no longer exists.

## II.

**We declare that building comes before understanding.** The traditional sequence — learn syntax, study structures, practice algorithms, then finally build something useful months later — is an inverted pedagogy. It mistakes the map for the territory. Vibes-first inverts the inversion: build something that works in minutes, verify it, understand what you need, go deeper when it matters. Seymour Papert knew this: "Better learning will not come from finding better ways for the teacher to instruct, but from giving the learner better opportunities to construct." We give them the construction site on day one.

## III.

**We declare that operational competence is real competence.** No one demands that drivers rebuild engines, that statisticians implement gradient descent, that editors parse every sentence of a manuscript before publication. We already live in a world of productive partial understanding — where literacy means knowing what matters, knowing what to check, and knowing when to call for help. We refuse the false binary of "full comprehension or fraud." Between those poles lies the vast territory where all real work happens.

## IV.

**We declare that verification is the new authorship.** When trust comes not from having written every line, but from having tested every claim — then testing *is* the craft. Specification literacy, verification literacy, debugging as hypothesis testing, selective reading as strategic intelligence — these are not lesser skills. They are the skills that matter when the machines can write but cannot judge. The human contribution is judgment. We train judgment.

## V.

**We declare that the humanities have been training for this moment all along.** The scholar who encounters a poem from a near-alien intelligence, who develops intuition before vocabulary, who tests interpretations against evidence, who builds understanding iteratively through encounter and re-encounter — that scholar already *knows* vibes-first methodology. Close reading becomes code review. Citation checking becomes edge-case testing. Interpretive charity becomes debugging. The artifact changes; the method is ancient.

## VI.

**We declare that the discomfort is the point.** "This feels like cheating." "I don't really understand what's happening." "This is too easy." Good. That discomfort is the felt edge of a new literacy forming. Manu Kapur called it *productive failure* — the struggle that activates prior knowledge, reveals gaps, and creates a felt need for concepts that instruction alone never could. We do not smooth the path. We make the struggle meaningful.

## VII.

**We declare that intuition is knowledge.** Michael Polanyi told us we know more than we can tell. Sherry Turkle and Seymour Papert showed us that the bricoleur — the tinkerer, the explorer, the one who stands back between brushstrokes to contemplate the canvas — has always been a valid knower. "Code taste" before code theory. The felt sense that something is wrong before the vocabulary to name it. This is not a failure of learning. It is the first stage of mastery. We honor the vibe.

## VIII.

**We declare that we have always been cyborgs.** Andy Clark was right: writing is external memory, maps are spatial reasoning, calculators are extended arithmetic. Socrates warned that writing would destroy memory and make minds lazy — and he was completely, magnificently wrong. The AI is the latest cognitive extension. Learning to orchestrate the distributed system of human intent, machine generation, runtime verification, and iterative refinement — this is not outsourcing thought. It is *extending* thought. We reject the myth of the autonomous, tool-free mind. That mind never existed.

## IX.

**We declare that the black box is not the enemy.** Latour taught us: "The more science and technology succeed, the more opaque they become." We do not open every box. We open the boxes that break, the boxes that matter, the boxes connected to high stakes. For the rest, we attend to inputs, outputs, and capabilities. "I don't understand the implementation" is not a confession. It is the *goal* of good abstraction. We refuse the cult of total transparency. We practice strategic opacity.

## X.

**We declare that vibe coding is not a shortcut — it is a different road.** Not coding made easy, but a different literacy for a different era. One that teaches specification clarity (thinking precisely about what you want), epistemic humility (knowing what you don't know), aesthetic judgment (sensing that something is off before you can say why), iterative refinement (treating every first attempt as a draft), attention to detail (reading outputs with a critical eye), and care — the moral dimension of working responsibly with powerful tools. These are not lesser skills. In a world of expanding opacity, they may be the skills that matter most.

## XI.

**We declare that the gatekeepers are wrong.** "This isn't real programming." "They won't really learn." "This is just cheating." These objections mistake one form of knowledge for all knowledge, one path to competence for the only path, one historical moment's skill set for an eternal requirement. When calculators arrived, they said the same thing. When word processors arrived, they said the same thing. When search engines arrived, they said the same thing. They were wrong every time. They are wrong now.

## XII.

**We declare that as AI-generated artifacts flood the world — code, text, images, analysis — the skills that become more valuable are precisely the skills we cultivate.** The ability to evaluate what you did not create. To notice what is wrong before you can articulate why. To hold multiple interpretations simultaneously. To distinguish genuine insight from fluent mediocrity. To build understanding from encounter rather than from first principles. The more powerful the generative tools become, the more the world needs people who can *judge* what those tools produce. The humanities have been training that judgment for centuries. We sharpen it and point it at code.

## XIII.

**We declare the vibes-first progression a universal method for navigating opacity:**

1. Encounter a complex artifact you do not fully understand.
2. Develop intuitions through interaction.
3. Test those intuitions against evidence.
4. Develop vocabulary to articulate what you notice.
5. Build understanding iteratively — deeper where it matters, abstracted where it does not.
6. Maintain epistemic humility: know what you have verified and what you are taking on trust.

This is not only a method for code. It is a method for the world we are entering — a world of systems that are increasingly powerful, increasingly capable, and increasingly opaque. The person who can work responsibly in those conditions is the person the future requires.

## XIV.

**We acknowledge what we might lose.** We are not reckless. Depth may erode if operational competence is always sufficient. Critical evaluation may weaken if the path of least resistance is to accept the first plausible output. Certain forms of knowledge may fade — the feel for the machine that only comes from building from nothing. These are real risks. We do not dismiss them. We design against them: building in the motivation for depth, cultivating the habit of verification, creating pathways from vibes to mastery for those who need them and want them.

## XV.

**We declare that this is a beginning, not an end.** Vibes-first is an on-ramp, not a destination. Week 1: everything feels strange. Week 3: patterns emerge. Month 2: selective understanding. Month 6: hybrid fluency — vibes for speed, tradition for depth, the best of both. We do not promise a complete education in ninety minutes. We promise that in ninety minutes you will have *built something that works*, and that the desire to understand it will carry you forward under your own power.

---

## Coda

We call on scholars, artists, humanists, scientists, students, and every curious mind excluded by the old gates:

**Specify boldly.** Say what you want with precision and ambition.

**Verify ruthlessly.** Trust nothing until you have tested it. Fluency is not truth.

**Iterate relentlessly.** The first draft is never the last. Neither is the fifth.

**Read strategically.** You do not need to understand every line. You need to understand the lines that matter.

**Go deeper when it matters.** Operational competence is the floor, not the ceiling.

**Be honest about what you know and what you don't.** Epistemic humility is not weakness. It is the highest form of intellectual rigor.

The tools are ready. The interfaces are ready. The need is urgent. The pedagogy is grounded. The vibes are, at last, correct.

**Now build something.**
