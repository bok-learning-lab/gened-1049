# Vibes-First Coding: A Pedagogical Defense

## What This Collection Argues

These documents make a case for teaching computational literacy through what Andrej Karpathy has called "vibe coding" — starting with AI-generated code, running it, verifying it works, and only then unpacking how and why. Building on Karpathy's term, a vibes-first approach reverses the traditional sequence: instead of learning syntax before building anything, you build something immediately, then learn what you need to know in order to trust, modify, and extend it.

The central claim is this: **the ability to work effectively with powerful systems you don't fully understand — specifying intent, verifying outcomes, iterating toward solutions — is a core literacy for the 21st century.** This literacy is not yet fully developed (even experts are still learning it), is not restricted to engineers, differs from traditional programming without being less rigorous, and draws on established pedagogical theory rather than improvisation. For humanities scholars in particular, it maps onto interpretive skills they already possess.

## The Documents

**[1. The Core Argument](01-core-argument.md)** develops the claim that vibes-first coding is not merely easier but fundamentally appropriate for this moment — that the bottleneck in computational work has shifted from writing code to verifying it, and that this shift calls for a different kind of literacy.

**[2. Learning Theory Foundations](02-learning-theory.md)** grounds the approach in education research: Papert's constructionism, Lave and Wenger's situated learning, Kapur's productive failure, and the Use-Modify-Create scaffold, among others.

**[3. Analogies That Land](03-analogies.md)** offers comparisons designed to resonate with humanities audiences — editorial judgment, driving without understanding engines, learning music by ear before theory, and the extended mind thesis.

**[4. Practical Strategies](04-practical-strategies.md)** provides concrete techniques for responsible vibe coding: specification as requirements writing, systematic verification, strategic code reading, and debugging as hypothesis testing.

**[5. Addressing Skeptics](05-addressing-skeptics.md)** responds to common objections — "this is just cheating," "they won't really learn," "LLMs make mistakes" — with evidence-based arguments that acknowledge legitimate concerns while identifying false dichotomies.

**[6. Vibes-First as Humanities Method](06-humanities-method.md)** argues that this approach is native to humanistic inquiry: encountering artifacts from near-alien intelligences, developing intuition before vocabulary, and building understanding iteratively through encounter and verification.

**[7. The Future of Vibes-First Learning](07-the-future.md)** looks ahead speculatively, arguing that the skills vibes-first coding develops — attention to detail, care, aesthetic judgment, specification clarity, epistemic humility, and iterative refinement — may be among the most important human skills in an era of increasingly powerful and opaque systems.

## Why This Matters

For students, understanding that this approach has intellectual grounding — not just convenience — helps them feel confident in their learning, defend their methods, and recognize when to go deeper. For faculty, theoretical backing supports pedagogical justification, course design, and institutional conversations. For skeptics, these documents offer evidence-based arguments, recognition of legitimate concerns, and a middle ground between "traditional only" and "AI everything."

## The Broader Context

Three converging realities make this approach both possible and necessary. First, natural language to working code is now reliable enough to be useful — the bottleneck has moved from writing to verifying, and AI assistance is improving, not plateauing. Second, computational literacy is increasingly necessary across disciplines, yet traditional programming education remains too slow and steep an on-ramp for many who need it. Third, we have well-established pedagogical theory — constructionism, productive failure, cognitive apprenticeship, situated learning — that supports exactly this kind of experience-first, theory-later progression. We are not inventing a new pedagogy. We are applying existing theory to new tools.

## How to Use These Documents

Workshop participants can read these if curious about the "why," share them with skeptical colleagues, or return to them when encountering resistance. Instructors can adapt the arguments for their context, reference them in course design, or use quotes in syllabi. Researchers can build on these arguments, conduct formal studies, or improve the approach through empirical validation.

## A Note on Terminology

"Vibe coding" can sound dismissive. Some prefer "AI-assisted programming," "specification-driven coding," or "verification-first programming." We use "vibes-first" because it captures the initial discomfort of working with incomplete understanding, because it was coined by a respected researcher (Karpathy), and because it honestly names the experience: you *are* following intuition at first. The underlying practice, however, is rigorous and grounded.
