# Analogies That Land

## Purpose

Analogies help humanities audiences connect vibes-first coding to domains they already understand. The comparisons here have been chosen to resonate with people who work with texts, interpret artifacts, and navigate complex systems without claiming to understand them completely.

## Close Reading vs. Editorial Judgment

As a scholar, you don't read every word of every source before using it. You develop editorial judgment: you sample key passages, check citations and evidence, triangulate across sources, know when to read closely versus skim, and build trust through strategic verification.

This is exactly how you work with AI-generated code. Close reading a key passage becomes reading critical code sections. Skimming for the general argument becomes understanding code structure. Checking citations becomes testing with known inputs and outputs. Triangulating sources becomes running multiple verification checks. Spotting weak evidence becomes detecting buggy logic.

The insight is that humanities scholars already work with texts they don't fully understand — yet. They build understanding selectively, focused on what matters. They trust incrementally, through verification. They know when to go deeper: high-stakes claims need scrutiny. You already have the core skill. You're applying it to a new artifact. And the reason this works is the same reason editorial judgment works: you'd never finish if you read every word, strategic sampling is efficient, you know what matters in your field, and you can check claims without reading everything. Selective attention is a feature, not a bug.

## Driving a Car

Most drivers can't explain fuel injection systems, transmission algorithms, or anti-lock braking systems. But they have operational competence: they interpret dashboard signals, hear when something sounds wrong, adapt to conditions, and know when to take the car to a mechanic.

No one says "you don't *really* know how to drive unless you can rebuild an engine," or "using power steering is cheating," or "automatic transmission drivers aren't real drivers." We accept that operational competence is different from implementation knowledge, and we don't confuse the two.

Literacy is fit for purpose. A commuter needs operational skills. A mechanic needs implementation knowledge. A race car driver needs both. Most people need operational coding skills — specify, verify, iterate. Few need implementation skills — write from scratch, optimize. The question isn't whether operational competence counts as "real" knowledge; it's whether it's sufficient for the task at hand.

## Statistics Without Implementing Algorithms

Social scientists run regression analyses without implementing optimization algorithms. They understand what the model assumes, how to interpret outputs, when results are trustworthy, and what to check for validity. They don't implement gradient descent, code matrix operations, or optimize computational efficiency. No one considers this fraudulent. No one says "you don't *really* understand statistics unless you code it from scratch" or "using R packages is cheating."

There are levels of abstraction — theory, implementation, application — and different roles need different levels. A research psychologist needs theory and application. A statistics software developer needs theory and implementation. Most people need application. The same structure applies to coding: most people need to use computational tools correctly, not implement them from scratch.

## Music by Ear Before Theory

Many successful musicians learned to play by ear before learning music theory. They imitated sounds, experimented with patterns, built intuition through practice, and learned theory later — to communicate what they already knew and to extend it.

There are two paths to musical literacy. Path A starts with reading music, studying scales and intervals, practicing exercises, and playing songs months later. Path B starts with playing a song you love (badly), practicing until it sounds right, and learning theory when you need it. Both reach musical literacy. Path B has higher motivation, because the thing you care about — making music — comes first.

Vibes-first is "play first, theory later." You build something that works (immediate motivation), develop intuition through interaction, learn theory on demand when you need to understand or extend, and deepen understanding iteratively. This isn't skipping fundamentals. It's reordering them.

## Black Boxes: When Complexity Disappears

Bruno Latour observed that when systems work reliably, they become "black-boxed" — their internal complexity becomes invisible. You don't think about TCP/IP when browsing the web. You don't consider sensor fusion when using GPS. You don't contemplate the supply chain when turning on a light. Attention shifts to inputs, outputs, and capabilities.

As Latour put it, "The more science and technology succeed, the more opaque they become." This is paradoxical but true: reliable systems hide complexity, users focus on what the system enables, and internal workings matter only when something breaks. AI code generation is now reliable enough to black-box for many uses.

"I don't understand the implementation" is not a failure state. It's the *goal* of good abstraction. You open the box when something breaks, when you need to modify it, when stakes are unusually high, or when you're genuinely curious. But you don't need to understand everything to use it responsibly.

## Extended Minds: We've Always Been Cyborgs

Andy Clark argues that humans have always extended cognition through tools. Writing in a notebook is external memory. Using a calculator is extended arithmetic. Navigating with GPS is augmented spatial reasoning. Searching Google is distributed knowledge. The "you" who solves problems isn't just your brain — it's brain plus tools.

The historical parallel is sharp. When writing was invented, Socrates objected: writing would create forgetfulness, because people would trust external characters rather than their own memories. This sounds reasonable. And it's completely wrong. Writing didn't destroy memory; it extended and transformed it. We remember different things (concepts rather than verbatim text), think new thoughts (enabled by external memory), and build on others' work across time and space.

Today, people object that AI coding will create ignorance, that students will trust the AI rather than their own understanding. This may be wrong for the same reasons. LLMs don't destroy understanding — they extend and transform it. We understand different things (architecture rather than syntax), think new thoughts (freed from implementation details), and build on AI capabilities beyond individual human speed.

The question isn't "Are you using AI as a crutch?" The question is "Are you using AI effectively as an extension?" Good extensions enable new capabilities, produce outputs you can verify, have limitations you understand. Bad extensions are blindly trusted, unchecked, and leave you helpless without them. Vibes-first teaches effective extension, not blind dependence.

## Tacit Knowledge: We Know More Than We Can Tell

Michael Polanyi observed that you can recognize your friend's face instantly but can't explain the algorithm, that you know when a melody sounds wrong but can't articulate the harmonic principle, that you ride a bike without being able to describe the physics. This is tacit knowledge — knowing-how without knowing-that.

In coding, this manifests as "this code smells wrong" before you can explain why, "this approach won't scale" as intuition from experience, "this pattern is risky" as a felt sense rather than formal analysis. You develop code taste before you can articulate principles.

The learning progression runs through four stages: unconscious incompetence ("I don't know what I don't know"), conscious incompetence ("I know I don't understand this"), conscious competence ("I can do it if I think carefully"), and unconscious competence ("I just know what works"). Traditional teaching tries to skip straight to conscious competence. Vibes-first embraces conscious incompetence as a productive learning state — the stage where you know enough to know what you don't know, which is exactly where learning happens. Formal knowledge comes later, making tacit knowledge explicit.

## Reading Code vs. Writing Code

Professional programmers spend far more time reading code than writing it — industry estimates suggest 70-80% reading, 20-30% writing. They're understanding existing systems, debugging, doing code review, integrating components, maintaining what others built. Yet traditional CS education inverts this: 90% writing from a blank page, 10% reading existing code.

Vibes-first prepares students for real-world coding practice. What matters professionally is not "write perfect code from scratch" but "work effectively in a codebase you don't fully understand." Can you read code strategically? Can you modify existing code? Can you integrate components? Can you verify correctness? Every professional developer does this daily. Vibes-first teaches it from day one.

## The Common Thread

All these analogies converge on one insight: competence does not require complete understanding. Literacy means appropriate competence for your context — commuter versus mechanic versus race car driver, reader versus editor versus author, user versus developer versus computer scientist. Different roles need different levels of understanding, and vibes-first gets you to operational competence fast, then supports deeper learning as needed.
