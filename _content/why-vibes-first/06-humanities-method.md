# Vibes-First as Humanities Method

## The Core Parallel

This document argues that vibes-first coding isn't merely *acceptable* for humanities students — it mirrors the fundamental method of humanistic inquiry itself. The way we approach AI-generated code is structurally identical to how we approach literature, music, and art.

When you encounter a poem, a symphony, or a painting, you are encountering the output of a near-alien intelligence. You don't have direct access to the creative process. You don't have the artist's conscious intentions (often unknown even to them). You don't have a technical manual explaining the work. You don't have a guarantee the work follows any rules. What you *do* have is the artifact itself, your embodied response to it, tools for testing interpretations, and a developing vocabulary for articulation. This is exactly how you work with AI-generated code.

## Kant on Genius: Making Without Knowing

In the *Critique of Judgment*, Kant describes genius as "the talent (natural gift) that gives the rule to art... the innate mental predisposition through which nature gives the rule to art." The key insight is that the genius doesn't know the rules they're following. A great poet cannot explain how they write a perfect line. They cannot teach the rules — only demonstrate. They create something rule-governed without conscious rule-following, and they produce work that others must learn to understand.

The AI is in the position of the genius. You are in the position of the critic. The AI generates code from learned patterns without knowing (in any human sense) what it's doing, produces syntactically correct, rule-governed artifacts, and operates as a black box whose process you can't observe. You, the vibe coder, receive the artifact, develop an initial sense of it, learn technical vocabulary to articulate what's happening, run tests and verifications, and build understanding iteratively.

You don't need to understand the artist's process to understand their art. You need careful attention to the artifact, methods for testing interpretations, vocabulary for articulating what's happening, and iterative refinement of understanding. This is already how you work.

## The Vibes-First Progression in Humanities

Consider how you learn to read literature. First, encounter and intuition: you read the text, feel something — confusion, delight, unease — and get a vibe: "this is sad," "this is ironic," "something's weird here." Then you attempt articulation: you try to say what's happening, struggle to find words, recognize patterns without naming them. Then technical vocabulary arrives: you learn terms like *iambic pentameter*, *enjambment*, *free indirect discourse*, and apply labels to felt experiences. Finally, mastery: you use technical vocabulary fluently, see patterns immediately, can teach others. You don't start with iambic pentameter. You start with "this rhythm feels like walking."

The same progression holds for music. You hear the piece and feel an emotional response. You notice "something happens here." You recognize that "it repeats but different" and "this part feels like it's resolving." You learn terms — *sonata form*, *modulation*, *counterpoint* — and map vocabulary onto intuitions that were already there. You don't start with sonata form. You start with "it goes away and comes back."

And for visual art. You look at the painting and notice what draws your eye. You feel spatial relationships. You struggle to describe: "the light is doing something," "this feels three-dimensional." You learn terms — *chiaroscuro*, *perspective*, *complementary colors* — and name what you've been seeing. You don't start with chiaroscuro. You start with "the shadows are really dramatic."

## The Pattern: Vibes, Then Vocabulary, Then Understanding

In every humanities discipline, the same progression holds. The artifact comes first — you encounter something made by an intelligence that can't fully explain itself, and you don't have a manual. Intuition precedes articulation — you feel, sense, and recognize patterns before you can name what you're experiencing. The vibe is real even without words. Vocabulary enables precision — technical terms aren't the starting point but tools for articulating what you already sense. They deepen perception but don't create it. Understanding is iterative — you return to the artifact with new vocabulary, see or hear or feel more each time, and mastery means rapid pattern recognition combined with precise articulation.

This is vibes-first methodology. And there's a reason technical vocabulary comes later in good pedagogy. "Here are 50 poetic devices — memorize them, then read a poem and identify them" is bad pedagogy. "Read this poem — what do you notice? What do you feel? Let's try to say what's happening... oh, that pattern has a name: *anaphora*" is good pedagogy. The same applies to programming. "Here are variable types, loops, and functions — memorize syntax, then build something" is bad pedagogy. "Here's working code — run it, what does it do? How could we modify it? Oh, that pattern has a name: *a loop*" is good pedagogy.

## AI as Artistic Intelligence

When you ask AI to generate code, you're in relationship with a system that produces rule-governed output without knowing (in any human sense) what it's doing, based on learned patterns from vast training, creating something you must interpret. This is structurally similar to a poet writing a perfect line without knowing the prosodic rules, a composer feeling a harmonic progression without analyzing the theory, a painter making spatial decisions without calculating perspective geometry.

And here's where code is actually *easier* than art in one crucial way: you can test it. Literary interpretations are debatable. Code correctness is verifiable. "Does this reading work?" is a question of argument and evidence. "Does this code work?" is a question you can answer by running it. You can verify code more definitively than you can verify literary interpretations. Run it — did it work? Test it — did it handle edge cases? Modify it — what changed? You have objective accountability that literary analysis often lacks.

## The Humanistic Skills You Already Have

Humanities training develops skills that transfer directly to working with code. *Pattern recognition*: in literature, you spot repeated images, phrases, structures and notice when something breaks a pattern; in code, you spot repeated logic and notice when something looks unusual. *Interpretive charity*: in literature, you assume the text is doing something intentional and look for coherence before declaring it broken; in code, you assume it might work as intended and test before declaring it broken. *Close versus distant reading*: in literature, you skim for general argument and close-read crucial passages; in code, you understand general structure and read critical sections carefully. *Contextual judgment*: in literature, high-stakes claims need more evidence and genre affects reading strategy; in code, high-stakes functions need more verification and use case affects testing strategy. *Building on incomplete understanding*: in literature, you write papers on books you don't fully understand and cite sources you haven't read completely; in code, you use code you don't fully understand and import libraries you haven't read completely. This is scholarly honesty, not fraud.

## Why This Matters for Humanities Students

First, you're not starting from zero. Traditional CS pedagogy often assumes you have no relevant skills, must learn everything from scratch, and that there's one right way to learn. This is false for humanities students, who bring sophisticated pattern recognition, interpretive skills, verification practices, comfort with partial understanding, and iterative meaning-making methods. Vibes-first leverages these existing strengths rather than ignoring them.

Second, this is intellectually rigorous. Some worry vibes-first is shallow, bypassing real learning. But this is exactly how you approach medieval manuscripts (you don't learn medieval orthography first), musical scores (you hear before you analyze), visual art (you look before you study art history), and foreign literature (you read translations before learning the language). In each case, you encounter the artifact first, build understanding iteratively, learn technical vocabulary as needed, and deepen over time. This is rigorous humanistic method, not a shortcut around it.

Third, it respects your expertise. Traditional programming courses implicitly say: forget everything you know, start from `print('Hello World')`, and trust us that you'll build something useful eventually. Vibes-first says: use the skills you already have, start by making something that works, and build understanding as you go. This respects intellectual maturity.

## "But Code Has Rules"

The objection is natural: code is different from art because it's strictly rule-governed. Either it compiles or it doesn't. You can't just "vibe" your way through syntax errors.

True — code has syntax rules. But so does poetry (prosodic rules), music (harmonic rules), and perspective drawing (geometric rules). In each case, the creator makes rule-governed artifacts without necessarily being conscious of the rules. The student learns rules after encountering examples. Mastery means internalized rules — unconscious competence. You don't learn iambic pentameter before reading Shakespeare. You read Shakespeare, notice the rhythm, then learn the rule has a name.

The deeper point: when you write a paper on a novel, you make claims (specify what you're arguing), provide evidence (verify your interpretation), revise when evidence doesn't fit (iterate), and don't understand everything about the novel (selective reading). This is the vibes-first method exactly: specify what you want, verify it works, iterate when it doesn't, understand selectively. The artifact is different — code versus novel. The method — specification, verification, iteration — is the same.

## The Scholarly Virtue: Knowing What You Don't Know

Good scholars acknowledge limitations of their argument, mark uncertainty, cite what they've verified, know when to defer to experts, and don't overstate their understanding. This is intellectual honesty. Good vibe coders do the same: acknowledge what they haven't verified, mark uncertainty about how code works, test what they need to trust, know when to ask for help, and don't overstate their understanding. Both are practices of epistemic humility — working with what you don't fully understand, being explicit about uncertainty, verifying what matters, and knowing your limitations. This is what responsible scholarship looks like, and what responsible vibe coding looks like.

## Conclusion

When you read a novel and write about it without understanding every literary device, when you listen to music and analyze it without knowing every harmonic principle, when you view art and interpret it without mastering every technique — you are encountering artifacts from near-alien intelligences, starting with vibes and intuitions, testing interpretations against evidence, learning vocabulary to articulate patterns, building understanding iteratively, and working responsibly with partial knowledge.

Vibes-first coding asks you to do exactly this with code. The alien intelligence is AI instead of human genius. The vocabulary is programming terms instead of literary terms. The verification is running code instead of citing textual evidence. But the fundamental method — encounter, intuit, articulate, verify, iterate — is identical. You already know how to do this. You've been trained for it. This is humanistic method applied to code.
