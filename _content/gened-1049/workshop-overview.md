# GENED 1049 Video Production Workshop

## Overview

This hands-on workshop introduces practical skills for creating video essays about East Asian Cinema. You'll rotate through three stations that build both your technical filmmaking knowledge and your ability to use AI tools to assist with media production workflows.

## Workshop Goals

By the end of this workshop, you'll be able to:

1. **Apply fundamental cinematography principles** to your own video projects
2. **Shoot dialogue scenes** using one-camera techniques and mobile devices
3. **Use AI tools and command-line utilities** to transform, analyze, and present video assets
4. **Envision the future of the video essay** as an interactive, web-based, multimodal form

## Station 1: Lighting

Learn to see like a cinematographer and make deliberate lighting choices for your video essays.

### Key Concepts
- **Three-point lighting**: key light, fill light, and back light working together
- **Key-to-fill ratio**: controlling contrast and mood
- **Practical lighting**: working with available light and repositioning subjects

### What You'll Do
- Take test shots with your phone before committing to camera placement
- Experiment with different angles and lighting positions
- Learn to work within constraints (dorm rooms, outdoor locations)

### Technical Terms
- **Key light**: Primary light source, creates main illumination and shadows
- **Fill light**: Softens shadows created by the key light
- **Back light**: Separates subject from background, adds depth

## Station 2: Shooting Dialogue

Master the techniques for capturing conversations and character interactions on camera.

### Key Concepts
- **180-degree rule**: Maintaining consistent screen direction and spatial relationships
- **Shot-reverse-shot**: The classic technique for filming conversations
- **One-camera shooting**: How to efficiently capture both sides of a dialogue

### What You'll Do
- Practice filming one actor's lines, then shooting coverage of the other actor
- Experiment with the two-phone technique: one for video, one as a microphone
- Learn how to position yourself and your equipment for consistent framing

### Pro Tip
Position a second phone between the two actors to capture better audio. Your phone's built-in mic picks up too much room noise—getting the mic closer makes a huge difference.

## Station 3: AI for Media Production

Discover how AI tools can accelerate technical workflows while you maintain creative control over your video essay's analysis and argument.

### What This Station is **For**
✅ **Technical assistance**: batch-processing files, writing command-line scripts, generating HTML/CSS for web presentations
✅ **Workflow automation**: downloading clips, extracting stills, creating gifs, compressing video
✅ **Future-forward thinking**: building interactive, scroll-synced video essays that live on the web

### What This Station is **Not For**
❌ **Conceptual help**: AI won't write your film analysis, develop your argument, or interpret scenes for you
❌ **Replacing your voice**: The critical thinking and interpretive work remains yours

### What You'll Learn

1. **Command-line tools** (`ffmpeg` and `yt-dlp`) for media manipulation
2. **Working with LLMs** to generate scripts and troubleshoot technical problems
3. **Building interactive video essays** that combine scrolling, video playback, and annotation

### Example Workflows

- Download a clip from YouTube in the right format for your editing software
- Extract stills from a video to analyze composition and framing
- Create gifs from specific moments for close reading
- Generate contact sheets to visualize the rhythm of a sequence
- Build web-based presentations where video responds to user scrolling

## Workshop Structure

- **5 minutes**: Introduction and overview
- **15 minutes per station**: Hands-on work (45 minutes total)
- **10 minutes**: Wrap-up, Q&A, and next steps
