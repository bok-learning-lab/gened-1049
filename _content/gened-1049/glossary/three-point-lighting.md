# Three-Point Lighting

## Definition

Three-point lighting is the foundational lighting setup in cinematography, consisting of three distinct light sources positioned around the subject: **key light**, **fill light**, and **back light**. Together, these lights create dimensional, controllable illumination that models the subject's features while separating them from the background.

## The Three Lights

### 1. Key Light
The primary light source, providing the main illumination and establishing the scene's dominant shadows. Typically the brightest of the three lights.

### 2. Fill Light
A softer, secondary light that reduces (but doesn't eliminate) the shadows created by the key light. Controls contrast and mood.

### 3. Back Light
Positioned behind the subject, this light creates a rim or halo effect that visually separates the subject from the background, adding depth to the image.

## Purpose

Three-point lighting serves multiple functions:
- **Dimensional modeling**: Creates depth by revealing form through highlights and shadows
- **Subject separation**: Distinguishes foreground from background
- **Mood control**: Adjusts emotional tone through the balance between lights
- **Professional polish**: Produces the controlled, intentional look characteristic of quality cinematography

## In Practice

While three-point lighting is the theoretical foundation, professional cinematographers adapt it constantly:
- You may use fewer lights (two-point lighting is common)
- Multiple lights can serve a single function (two fill lights, for example)
- Natural/practical light sources can replace artificial lights
- The key is understanding the *function* each light serves, not rigidly following a formula

## Key-to-Fill Ratio

The relationship between the key light's brightness and the fill light's brightness determines contrast and mood. A **high key-to-fill ratio** (strong key, weak fill) creates dramatic shadows and tension. A **low key-to-fill ratio** (key and fill similar in brightness) creates even, soft lighting often used in comedy or optimistic scenes.

## Historical Context

Three-point lighting emerged from Hollywood studio era cinematography (1930s-1950s), when large soundstages with extensive lighting grids allowed precise control. The technique remains relevant because it codifies fundamental principles of how light reveals form and creates visual hierarchy—principles that apply whether you're using professional film lights or positioning an actor near a window.
