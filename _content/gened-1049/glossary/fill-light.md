# Fill Light

## Definition

The **fill light** is a secondary light source that softens or reduces the shadows created by the key light. It doesn't eliminate shadows entirely—that would create flat, dimensionless lighting—but rather controls the depth and intensity of shadows to achieve the desired contrast and mood.

## Function

Fill light serves several purposes:
- **Controls contrast**: Reduces the darkness of shadows cast by the key light
- **Reveals detail**: Makes shadow areas visible rather than completely black
- **Sets mood**: The strength of fill light relative to the key light determines whether a scene feels dramatic (strong shadows) or gentle (soft shadows)
- **Maintains dimension**: Properly balanced fill keeps the image three-dimensional while preventing overly harsh contrast

## Placement

Fill light is typically positioned:
- **Opposite the key light**: If the key is frame right, fill is frame left
- **At or near camera height**: Roughly level with the subject's face, not high above
- **Close to camera axis**: Often placed near the camera itself to fill in shadows directly visible to the lens

The fill light should be **soft and diffused** rather than hard and direct, preventing it from creating its own harsh shadow patterns.

## Fill Light Intensity

The brightness of the fill light determines the scene's contrast. This is measured as the **key-to-fill ratio**:

- **High key-to-fill ratio** (e.g., 8:1, 4:1): Weak fill, strong shadows → dramatic, high-contrast, film noir
- **Low key-to-fill ratio** (e.g., 2:1, 1:1): Strong fill, soft shadows → flat, even, sitcom or high-key comedy

## Sources of Fill Light

Fill light can come from:
- **Dedicated fill lamp**: A separate light specifically serving this function
- **Reflectors**: Bounce boards that redirect key light back into shadows (common in location shooting)
- **Environmental bounce**: Light reflecting off walls, ceilings, or nearby surfaces
- **Practical sources**: Windows, lamps, or other in-scene light sources positioned to act as fill
- **Ambient light**: Overall environmental illumination serving as natural fill

## Fill Without a Light

Low-budget productions often create fill light by:
- **White foam core boards**: Reflect key light back onto the subject
- **Walls and ceilings**: Position subject near reflective surfaces
- **Natural light**: Use window light as key, room as natural fill
- **Negative fill**: Instead of adding fill, remove ambient light with black fabric to increase contrast

## Quality Matters

Fill light should be **diffuse** and **non-directional**:
- **Soft and spread**: Large source or heavily diffused
- **No sharp shadows**: Shouldn't create competing shadow patterns
- **Gentle transition**: Gradual falloff from lit to shadow areas

If your fill light creates its own distinct shadows, it's too hard or too bright—it's competing with the key rather than supporting it.

## Common Mistakes

- **Too much fill**: Eliminates all shadows, creating flat, dimensionless lighting
- **Hard fill**: Creates its own shadows that fight with key light shadows
- **Mismatched color**: Fill light has different color temperature than key (unless intentional for effect)
- **Visible in shot**: Fill light or reflector appears in frame

## No Fill = Low Key Lighting

Deliberately omitting fill light creates **low-key lighting**, characterized by:
- Deep, rich shadows
- High contrast
- Dramatic mood
- Film noir aesthetic
- Horror/thriller atmosphere

This is a valid creative choice when mood is more important than visibility in shadow areas.

## Historical Context

Studio-era Hollywood (1940s-1950s) typically used strong fill light to ensure every part of the star's face was visible—glamour required no unflattering shadows. Contemporary cinema often uses less fill, allowing deeper shadows for naturalism or drama.
