# Back Light

## Definition

The **back light** (also called **rim light**, **hair light**, or **separation light**) is positioned behind the subject, creating a rim or halo of light around the edges of the subject's silhouette. This light separates the subject from the background, adding depth and dimension to the image.

## Function

Back light serves several crucial purposes:
- **Subject separation**: Distinguishes foreground from background, preventing the subject from visually merging with the environment
- **Depth creation**: Adds a sense of three-dimensionality to what would otherwise be a flat image
- **Visual interest**: Creates attractive highlights on hair, shoulders, or profile edges
- **Professional polish**: Adds the "finished" quality characteristic of intentional cinematography

## Placement

Back light is typically positioned:
- **Behind the subject**: Directly opposite the camera, or slightly to one side
- **High angle**: Often elevated to hit the top of the head and shoulders
- **Out of frame**: Must be carefully placed to avoid lens flare or appearing in shot

The light should hit the edges of the subject without directly shining into the camera lens (unless lens flare is desired for effect).

## Back Light Intensity

Back light intensity varies based on:
- **Subject's coloring**: Dark hair requires stronger back light than blonde hair to achieve visible separation
- **Background darkness**: Darker backgrounds need stronger back light; light backgrounds need less
- **Desired effect**: Subtle rim vs. dramatic halo
- **Genre conventions**: Music videos and glamour photography often use very strong back light; naturalistic drama uses more subtle back light

Typically, back light is brighter than fill light but can range from equal to the key light to much brighter, depending on desired effect.

## Types of Back Light

### Hair Light
Specifically targets the top of the head, creating highlights in hair. Common in portrait and beauty cinematography.

### Rim Light
Creates a thin line of light along the entire edge of the subject's silhouette. Often used in dramatic or high-style cinematography.

### Kicker
An edge light positioned at a 3/4 back angle (between side and back), creating a highlight on one side of the subject. Often used to add sparkle to cheekbones or add definition to profile shots.

### Practical Back Light
A visible light source in the frame (lamp, window, candle) positioned behind the subject, serving both as set dressing and functional back light.

## Color and Quality

Back light can be:
- **Same color as key light**: Creates cohesive, unified lighting
- **Different color**: Adds visual interest or separates time periods/locations (warm key with cool back light is common)
- **Hard or soft**: Hard back light creates a defined rim; soft back light creates a subtle glow

## Without Back Light

Omitting back light results in:
- **Flat depth**: Subject and background visually compress together
- **Lost detail**: Dark-haired subjects against dark backgrounds become silhouetted
- **Lower production value**: Image can feel less professional or intentional
- **Naturalistic look**: Some documentary or naturalistic styles deliberately avoid back light for a more raw aesthetic

## Common Mistakes

- **Too bright**: Overwhelming rim that draws attention to the lighting rather than the subject
- **Lens flare**: Back light shining directly into lens, creating unwanted flares or reduced contrast
- **Inconsistent angle**: Back light direction changes between shots, breaking continuity
- **Visible in frame**: Back light or its stand appears in the shot

## Practical Tips

When working with limited equipment:
- **Use windows**: Position subject with window behind them for natural back light
- **Small lights work well**: Back lights don't need to be large or powerful—a small LED can create effective edge lighting
- **Flags and barn doors**: Use these to control where back light falls, preventing lens flare
- **Check from camera**: Always view back light's effect through the actual camera, not just from the light's position

## Historical Context

Back lighting became standard in Hollywood's golden age (1930s-1950s) when cinematographers like Gregg Toland developed sophisticated multi-light techniques. The technique distinguished professional studio work from lower-budget productions that used simpler two-light or one-light setups.

## Genre Variations

- **Film noir**: Strong, dramatic back light creating pronounced halos
- **Horror**: Selective back light creating ominous silhouettes
- **Glamour/music videos**: Intense, colorful back lights for stylization
- **Documentary/realism**: Minimal or no back light for naturalistic look
- **Period drama**: Motivated back light from practical sources (windows, candles, lamps)
