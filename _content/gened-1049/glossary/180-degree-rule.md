# 180-Degree Rule

## Definition

The 180-degree rule is a fundamental principle of continuity editing that maintains consistent screen direction and spatial relationships between shots. It states that the camera should stay on one side of an imaginary axis (line) drawn between two subjects throughout a scene. Crossing this line disorients viewers by flipping the spatial relationships on screen.

## How It Works

1. **Establish the axis**: Draw an imaginary line between two characters in conversation, or along a character's direction of movement
2. **Keep camera on one side**: All shots in the scene should be filmed from the same side of this line
3. **Maintain screen direction**: If Character A is on frame left and Character B is on frame right in one shot, this relationship must remain consistent across all shots in the scene

## Why It Matters

The 180-degree rule creates **spatial continuity**—viewers intuitively understand where each character or object exists in the scene's geography. Breaking the rule (called "crossing the line" or "jumping the line") can:
- Confuse viewers about who's talking to whom
- Make characters appear to switch positions unexpectedly
- Create the impression that characters are facing the same direction rather than toward each other

## Visual Example

```
     CAMERA ZONE (180 degrees)
            /‾‾‾‾‾\
           /       \
          |    B    |
          |         |
    ------●---------●------ (THE LINE / AXIS)
          |         |
          |    A    |
           \       /
            \_____/

     CAMERA SHOULD STAY IN THIS HEMISPHERE
```

If Camera 1 shoots Character A from this side of the line, all subsequent shots must maintain the same side. Crossing to the opposite hemisphere flips everyone's positions on screen.

## Shot-Reverse-Shot

The 180-degree rule is especially crucial in **shot-reverse-shot** editing, the standard technique for filming conversations. Each "reverse" shot must maintain the axis, ensuring that Character A always looks frame right at Character B, and B always looks frame left at A.

## When to Break It

Intentional violations of the 180-degree rule can:
- Signal disorientation, confusion, or psychological breakdown
- Mark a narrative shift or time jump
- Create dynamic energy in action sequences (though this requires skill to avoid mere confusion)

However, breaking the rule should be a deliberate creative choice, not an accident. Most professional filmmaking maintains the 180-degree rule throughout dialogue and continuity scenes.

## Neutral Shots

A shot directly on the axis (looking straight down the line between characters) is called a **neutral shot**. It doesn't favor either side and can be used to transition between angles without crossing the line.

## Practical Application

When shooting dialogue:
1. Position yourself on one side of your actors
2. Shoot all of Character A's angles from this position
3. Shoot all of Character B's angles from the same side (not from opposite side)
4. In editing, the characters will maintain consistent screen positions

## Historical Context

The 180-degree rule emerged alongside continuity editing in early Hollywood cinema (1910s-1930s). D.W. Griffith and other pioneers discovered that maintaining spatial consistency across cuts created seamless narrative flow. The rule became standard practice because it made invisible editing possible—viewers could follow the story without consciously noticing the cuts.
