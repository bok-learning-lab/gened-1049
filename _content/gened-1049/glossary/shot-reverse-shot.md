# Shot-Reverse-Shot

## Definition

**Shot-reverse-shot** is the fundamental editing pattern for filming conversations and character interactions. The technique cuts back and forth between characters, typically showing one character's point of view, then reversing to show the other character's point of view, while maintaining the 180-degree rule for spatial continuity.

## How It Works

The basic pattern:
1. **Shot A**: Character A speaking or listening, looking frame right at Character B
2. **Shot B** (the "reverse"): Character B speaking or listening, looking frame left at Character A
3. Repeat: Cut back to Character A, then B, alternating as dialogue continues

This creates the illusion that we're watching a continuous conversation, even though each side was likely filmed separately.

## Why It's Fundamental

Shot-reverse-shot is cinema's basic vocabulary for dialogue because it:
- **Mimics natural attention**: We look back and forth between speakers in real conversations
- **Shows both speakers**: Unlike theatrical staging where we see both actors simultaneously
- **Emphasizes reactions**: Allows us to see how each character responds to what the other says
- **Maintains spatial clarity**: When executed correctly, viewers always know where each character is positioned
- **Edits invisibly**: The pattern is so familiar that audiences don't consciously notice the cuts

## Typical Shot Sizes

Shot-reverse-shot often uses matching shot sizes:
- **Medium shots** (MS): Waist to head, showing body language and facial expressions
- **Medium close-ups** (MCU): Shoulders to head, emphasizing face
- **Close-ups** (CU): Face fills frame, maximum emotional intimacy

Conversations often start with wider shots and gradually tighten as emotional intensity builds—a technique called **progressive intensification**.

## Over-the-Shoulder Shots

A common variation includes the back of one character's head and shoulder in the foreground, with the other character in focus behind them. This **over-the-shoulder** (OTS) shot:
- Provides more spatial context than singles
- Emphasizes the relationship between characters
- Makes the reverse shot feel more connected
- Allows for eyeline matching

## The 180-Degree Rule

Shot-reverse-shot requires maintaining the **180-degree rule**:
- Camera stays on one side of the imaginary axis between characters
- Character A always looks frame right, Character B always looks frame left
- Breaking this rule (crossing the line) flips screen positions and disorients viewers

## Single-Camera Shooting

Most shot-reverse-shot sequences are filmed with one camera, requiring multiple takes:

1. **Master shot**: Wide shot showing both characters for spatial reference
2. **Character A coverage**: Film all of A's dialogue and reactions from one camera position
3. **Character B coverage**: Film all of B's dialogue and reactions from the reverse position
4. **Editing**: Assemble the conversation by cutting between A and B angles

This means actors must match their performance across multiple takes to maintain continuity.

## Variations

### Dutch Angle Reverse Shot
Tilted camera creates unease or disorientation (horror, psychological drama)

### Split Diopter
Both characters in focus despite different distances (De Palma, Tarantino use this)

### Vertical Stack
One character on phone, other elsewhere—edited as if they're in conversation

### Eyeline Mismatch
Deliberately mismatching eyelines to suggest disconnection or miscommunication

## When Not to Use It

Some directors deliberately avoid shot-reverse-shot to create specific effects:
- **Long takes**: Wes Anderson, Paul Thomas Anderson use sustained wide shots instead
- **Ensemble blocking**: Robert Altman keeps multiple characters in frame simultaneously
- **POV distortion**: First-person perspectives (found footage, video game adaptations)
- **Alienation effects**: Brechtian cinema avoiding conventional identification

## Common Mistakes

- **Crossing the line**: Breaking 180-degree rule and flipping character positions
- **Mismatched eyelines**: Characters looking wrong direction, appearing to look past each other
- **Mismatched lighting**: Key light direction changes between angles, breaking continuity
- **Jump cuts**: Cutting between similar angles without enough visual difference
- **Poor performance matching**: Actor's energy or emotion inconsistent across takes

## The "Singles" vs. "Two-Shot"

- **Singles**: Each character in a separate frame (classic shot-reverse-shot)
- **Two-shot**: Both characters in the same frame
- **OTS (Over-the-Shoulder)**: One character's back in foreground, other in background

Effective dialogue editing mixes these, using two-shots for context and singles for emotional emphasis.

## Emotional Escalation

As scenes intensify, editors typically:
1. **Start wider**: Establish geography with wider shots
2. **Tighten progressively**: Move to MCU, then CU as emotion builds
3. **Cut faster**: Increase cutting pace during conflict or climax
4. **Hold longer**: Sustain on one character during crucial revelations

This visual rhythm matches the emotional rhythm of the scene.

## Historical Context

Shot-reverse-shot emerged in early silent cinema (1910s) as editors discovered how cutting between angles created psychological engagement impossible in theater. D.W. Griffith and Soviet montage theorists (Kuleshov, Eisenstein) established that meaning emerges from the *relationship* between shots, not just their content. Shot-reverse-shot became cinema's primary dialogue technique by the 1920s and remains dominant today.

## Cultural Variations

While shot-reverse-shot is global standard, some national cinemas use it differently:
- **Classical Hollywood**: Fast-paced, many cuts, tight framing
- **European art cinema**: Longer takes, wider framing, fewer cuts
- **Asian cinema**: Sometimes incorporates theatrical staging, less reliance on cutting
- **Contemporary streaming**: Often uses longer takes inspired by prestige TV
