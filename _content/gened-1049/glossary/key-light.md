# Key Light

## Definition

The **key light** is the primary light source in a lighting setup—the brightest and most dominant light that establishes the scene's main illumination, creates the principal shadows, and determines the overall lighting direction and mood.

## Function

The key light serves several crucial purposes:
- **Main illumination**: Provides the primary visibility of the subject
- **Shadow creation**: Establishes where shadows fall, which defines form and depth
- **Directional cue**: Tells viewers where light is "coming from" in the scene's world
- **Mood establishment**: The key light's angle, intensity, and quality set the emotional tone

## Placement

Common key light positions include:

### 45-Degree Angle (Classic Position)
Positioned 45 degrees to the side and 45 degrees above the subject. Creates natural-looking shadows with good facial modeling. This is the "default" position taught in film schools.

### Frontal Key
Placed directly in front of the subject, creating flat, even lighting with minimal shadows. Often used in beauty lighting, fashion photography, or high-key comedy.

### Side Key (90 Degrees)
Positioned at a right angle to the subject, illuminating only half the face. Creates dramatic split lighting (also called "hatchet lighting") often used in film noir or thriller genres.

### High Key (Top Light)
Positioned directly above the subject, creating shadows under eyes, nose, and chin. Can feel ominous, harsh, or documentary-realistic depending on context.

### Low Key (Under-lighting)
Light from below the face—an unnatural angle that typically feels eerie or sinister because it reverses the shadows we expect from overhead light sources (sun, ceiling lights).

## Key Light Quality

### Hard Light
- Creates sharp, defined shadow edges
- Produced by small, direct light sources (sun, bare bulbs, focused spotlights)
- Feels dramatic, harsh, or high-contrast
- Reveals texture in skin and surfaces

### Soft Light
- Creates gradual, diffused shadow transitions
- Produced by large or diffused light sources (overcast sky, softboxes, bounce light)
- Feels gentle, flattering, or naturalistic
- Minimizes skin texture and imperfections

## Intensity

The key light is typically the brightest light in your setup, but its intensity relative to other lights determines the scene's contrast and mood. This relationship is measured as the **key-to-fill ratio**.

## In Practice

Professional cinematographers rarely use "perfect" key light placement. Instead, they:
- **Motivate the key light**: Make it appear to come from a believable source (window, lamp, fire)
- **Adjust for face shape**: Modify angle to flatter or characterize the subject
- **Respond to genre**: Horror uses different key light angles than romantic comedy
- **Consider narrative**: Key light direction can shift within a scene to reflect emotional changes

## Common Mistakes

- **Over-lighting**: Making the key too bright, eliminating all shadows and creating a flat image
- **Undermotivated key**: Lighting that doesn't correspond to any visible source, breaking immersion
- **Neglecting key direction consistency**: Changing key light direction between shots breaks continuity

## Historical Note

The term "key light" comes from theater and early film lighting, where the "key" was the main lantern that needed to be properly positioned for the scene to work. All other lights were support or "fill" for this key source.
